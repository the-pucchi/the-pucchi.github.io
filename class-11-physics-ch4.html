Chapter 4 ‚Äî Motion in a Plane

You've mastered motion in a straight line. But the world isn't one-dimensional. Balls fly through the air in curves. Cars turn corners. Planets orbit stars. This is where vectors come in ‚Äî and where physics gets really interesting.

1. Scalars and Vectors

Some quantities just need a number. Temperature: 25¬∞C. Mass: 5 kg. Speed: 10 m/s. These are scalars ‚Äî they have magnitude (size) but no direction.

But try describing the wind. "10 m/s" isn't enough ‚Äî you need to know which way it's blowing. That's a vector ‚Äî it has both magnitude and direction.

Examples:

  • Scalars: temperature, mass, distance, speed, time, energy
  • Vectors: displacement, velocity, force, acceleration, momentum

We draw vectors as arrows. The length of the arrow shows magnitude. The direction of the arrow shows... well, direction. We write them in bold (A) or with an arrow on top (A‚Éó).

Two vectors are equal only if both their magnitude and direction match. Same length, same direction, same vector.

Scalars add like normal numbers. 5 kg + 3 kg = 8 kg. But vectors don't ‚Äî direction matters. That's what makes them interesting.

2. Addition of Vectors ‚Äî Triangle Law

Here's the thing: you can't just add vector magnitudes. If you walk 3 meters north, then 4 meters east, you didn't travel 7 meters total ‚Äî you're ‚àö(3¬≤ + 4¬≤) = 5 meters from where you started. Direction matters.

The Triangle Law gives us the rule: to add vectors A and B, place the tail of B at the head (tip) of A. The resultant R goes from the tail of A to the head of B, closing the triangle.

Mathematically: R = A + B

Vector addition is commutative: A + B = B + A. It doesn't matter which vector you place first ‚Äî the resultant is the same. Let's prove it.
