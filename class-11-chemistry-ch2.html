Structure of Atom

Chapter 2 ‚Äî How atoms actually work

1. The Bohr Model ‚Äî Electrons in Orbits

In 1913, Niels Bohr proposed a revolutionary model: electrons orbit the nucleus in fixed energy levels, like planets around the sun. But unlike planets, electrons can only exist in specific orbits ‚Äî not anywhere in between.

Key ideas:

  • Electrons orbit in discrete energy levels (n = 1, 2, 3...)
  • Each level has a fixed energy ‚Äî electrons can't exist between levels
  • When electrons jump down to a lower level, they emit light (a photon)
  • When electrons absorb energy, they jump up to a higher level
  • The energy of the emitted photon = E‚ÇÇ - E‚ÇÅ (difference between levels)

This model explained the line spectra of hydrogen ‚Äî why atoms emit light at specific wavelengths, not a continuous spectrum. Each line corresponds to an electron transition between two energy levels.

2. Quantum Numbers ‚Äî The Electron's Address

The Bohr model was a start, but it couldn't explain atoms with multiple electrons. Quantum mechanics revealed that electrons aren't just in orbits ‚Äî they exist in orbitals, which are 3D regions of probability. Each electron has a unique set of four quantum numbers:

  • n (principal): Energy level (1, 2, 3...) ‚Äî determines size and energy
  • l (azimuthal): Orbital shape (0=s, 1=p, 2=d, 3=f) ‚Äî determines angular momentum
  • ml (magnetic): Orbital orientation (-l to +l) ‚Äî determines orientation in space
  • ms (spin): Electron spin (+¬Ω or -¬Ω) ‚Äî intrinsic angular momentum

Think of it as an address: n is the floor, l is the room type, ml is which specific room, and ms is which bed in the room. No two electrons can have identical quantum numbers (Pauli Exclusion Principle).

Number of orbitals per subshell:

  • s subshell (l=0): 1 orbital, holds 2 electrons
  • p subshell (l=1): 3 orbitals, holds 6 electrons
  • d subshell (l=2): 5 orbitals, holds 10 electrons
  • f subshell (l=3): 7 orbitals, holds 14 electrons

3. Electron Configuration ‚Äî Filling the Orbitals

Electrons fill orbitals in a specific order, following three rules:

  • Aufbau Principle: Fill lowest energy orbitals first (1s, then 2s, then 2p, then 3s...)
  • Pauli Exclusion: Max 2 electrons per orbital, with opposite spins
  • Hund's Rule: Fill orbitals singly before pairing up (minimizes repulsion)

The filling order isn't always sequential! After 3p, we fill 4s before 3d, because 4s is lower in energy. The order is: 1s, 2s, 2p, 3s, 3p, 4s, 3d, 4p, 5s, 4d, 5p, 6s, 4f, 5d, 6p...

A helpful trick: Draw diagonal arrows through the periodic table. Follow the arrows to get the filling order.
